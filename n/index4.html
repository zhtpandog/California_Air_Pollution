Introduction

California is known to have the most polluted air in the US. In a 2011 survey done by Public Policy Institute of California, about 45% of the Los Angeles surveyees showed concern about air pollution. While most of us do not have direct control over the issue, as residents of CA, it is still important for us to have a better understanding of the current air pollution situation in order to make wiser decisions for ourselves, our family and our society. This website aims to provide not only a real-time dashboard of air pollution readings but also a thorough analysis of pollution data from the past year. We carefully selected data from 14 monitor stations located in the most populated areas in CA. The visualizations presented will be relevant to both environmentalists who study the issue in depth as well as commoners who just want to be a little more informed.

Data Processing

Click on each tab to learn more about our data processing.


SELECTION

Our primary data comes from the Pre-Generated Data Files of U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)'s Air Data. We selected data of 2016 from Hourly Data to ensure a detail analysis including seasonality. While the original data set contains hourly reading from each of the monitoring stations around the U.S., we only kept records that belong to one of the 14 stations that monitor all four pollutants (CO, NO2, Ozone, SO2) in California. We further simplified the data source by dropping unnecessary fields and only focused on time and location components.

STANDARDIZATION

The orignal data contains raw readings of each pollutant. However, if we simply compare the raw readings of different pollutants, the conclusion we get might be biased since different pollutants have different standards for what can be considered as serious pollution. For example, while 1 ppm of CO might not be a serious issue, 1 ppm of Ozone is definitely killing. In order to be able to compare different pollutants fairly, we standardized the data by taking the raw readings and dividing them by the pollutions standards of the respective pollutants. (The pollution standard comes from EPA's NAAQS Table. With the division, we converted all raw readings into comparable indexes. A value of 1.2 now means that the reading exceeds the pollution standard by 20%, and a value of 0.5 means that the reading is halfway (50%) to the pollution standard.

SUMMARIZATION

The level of details given by the hourly data gives us enough flexibility to summarize the data based on different dimensions. The summary levels include season, month, day of week, locations, as well as different combinations of the above. The majority of our graphs visualize different summary data to provide a comprehensive view.
